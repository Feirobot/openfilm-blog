## Introduction

Beyond what your eyes can see, there exists an entirely different world of light.

The visible spectrum for the human eye ranges roughly from 380nm to 700nm — electromagnetic radiation beyond this range is neither visible nor perceptible to us. But in the world of film, there are special emulsions capable of "seeing" infrared light at wavelengths of 700nm and even beyond 900nm. The photographs it produces are otherworldly: green foliage turns snow-white, blue skies become deep as ink, and skin takes on an almost surreal texture. This is a world both familiar and alien — a hidden dimension that only infrared film can reveal.

Infrared photography is no invention of the digital age. As early as the 1930s, scientists were using infrared film to record atmospheric and geological data. In the 1960s, photographers discovered its artistic potential, using it to create dreamlike, surreal imagery. Today, even though digital infrared conversions have become quite mature, infrared film continues to attract countless film photographers with its unique tonal response, unpredictable imaging characteristics, and purely chemical process.

This article will take you from zero to confident infrared film photographer, covering every aspect of the craft.

## The Nature of Infrared Light: Why It's So Special

### The Invisible Spectrum

Infrared light falls between visible red light and microwaves on the electromagnetic spectrum. It is typically divided into near-infrared (NIR, 700-1400nm), mid-infrared (MIR), and far-infrared (FIR). Film photography primarily uses the **near-infrared** range.

Infrared light differs from visible light in several key physical properties:

- **Less scattering**: Infrared light has longer wavelengths and is scattered less by atmospheric particles. This is why distant objects appear remarkably clear in infrared photographs — atmospheric haze affects infrared light far less than visible light
- **Different penetration**: Infrared light can pass through materials that are opaque to visible light, such as thin fog and certain plastics
- **The Wood Effect in vegetation**: Healthy plant chlorophyll strongly reflects infrared light (reflectance can exceed 50%), a phenomenon known as the Wood Effect. This is why foliage appears bright white in infrared photographs

### How Film Senses Infrared Light

The light-sensitive emulsion of traditional black-and-white film consists of silver halide crystals (primarily silver bromide) suspended in gelatin. These crystals are naturally sensitive to blue and ultraviolet light, but by adding sensitizing dyes, their sensitivity can be extended to different wavelengths.

Panchromatic film covers the entire visible spectrum through sensitizing dyes. Infrared film goes further, incorporating special dyes that respond to near-infrared wavelengths, enabling the emulsion to record light at 700nm and beyond.

## Choosing Your Infrared Film

The following infrared films are currently available:

### Ilford SFX 200

Ilford SFX 200 is the most accessible and beginner-friendly infrared film available. Strictly speaking, it is an **extended red sensitivity** film rather than a true infrared film.

- **ISO**: 200 (panchromatic mode); requires significant compensation when used with infrared filters
- **Spectral response**: Extends to approximately 740nm — redder than panchromatic film, but not as far as true infrared films
- **Characteristics**: Produces visible infrared effects even without a dedicated IR filter; a deep red filter (such as Heliopan 715nm) yields partial infrared effects; an R72 filter produces stronger infrared rendering
- **Processing**: Standard black-and-white chemistry (ID-11, D-76, etc.), no special handling required
- **Availability**: Low — sold by all major film retailers

Ilford SFX 200 has wide exposure latitude. Even without an infrared filter, a simple deep red filter can produce a noticeable "infrared look" — brighter foliage, darker skies. This is a major reason it has become the go-to choice for beginners.

### Rollei Infrared 400

Rollei Infrared 400 is a true infrared film with spectral response extending to approximately 820nm.

- **ISO**: 400 (unfiltered); approximately ISO 3-6 with an R72 filter
- **Spectral response**: Extends to approximately 820nm, capable of deeper infrared effects than SFX 200
- **Characteristics**: Stronger infrared rendering than SFX 200; foliage appears whiter
- **Processing**: Standard black-and-white chemistry
- **Availability**: Medium — available from select specialist retailers

Note that Rollei Infrared 400 uses a thicker PET base, which may curl in certain processing tanks. Pay special attention to how you load the film.

### Kodak Aerochrome / Aerochrome II

Kodak Aerochrome is a legendary **infrared color reversal film**, originally designed for military and aerial survey use.

- **ISO**: 200 (unfiltered)
- **Spectral response**: Extends to approximately 900nm
- **Characteristics**: Produces the iconic false-color effect — foliage appears red to magenta, blue areas remain blue or turn dark, and the overall image looks like an alien landscape
- **Processing**: C-41 or E-6 process (depending on specific variant)
- **Availability**: Extremely high — discontinued in 2007; expired rolls command high prices and storage conditions are uncertain

Aerochrome is the "holy grail" of infrared photography. Its false-color effect — red foliage, deep blue skies, magenta water — has become the most iconic visual signature of infrared photography. In recent years, as expired Aerochrome prices have continued to climb, some resellers have begun cutting down expired Aerochrome motion picture film into 135 or 120 formats.

### Other Options

- **Kodak HIE (High Speed Infrared)**: Another classic infrared black-and-white film with spectral response extending to approximately 900nm, discontinued in 2007. Expired rolls occasionally appear on the second-hand market, but storage conditions are impossible to verify
- **JCH Street Pan 400**: Some photographers report a degree of infrared response when used with deep red filters, though it is not officially marketed as an infrared film

## Infrared Filters: Choosing Your "Window"

Infrared filters are the essential accessory for infrared photography. Their function is to **block visible light and allow only infrared light to pass**, ensuring the film records only infrared information.

### Common Infrared Filter Types

| Filter Model | Pass Band | Effect Strength | Exposure Compensation | Use Case |
|-------------|-----------|----------------|----------------------|----------|
| Heliopan 715nm | 715nm+ | Moderate | ~2-3 stops | SFX 200 entry-level, retains some visible light |
| Hoya R72 | 720nm+ | Strong | ~11-13 stops | Standard infrared photography, most popular |
| Hoya R830 (B+W 093) | 830nm+ | Extreme | ~15-17 stops | Pure infrared effect, requires long exposure |
| Kolari Vision IR 720nm | 720nm+ | Strong | ~11-13 stops | Similar to Hoya R72 |
| Kolari Vision IR 850nm | 850nm+ | Extreme | ~15+ stops | Pure infrared, most extreme effect |

**The R72 is the recommended starting filter.** It offers the best balance between infrared effect and shootability — the effect is strong enough, and the exposure compensation is manageable (about 11-13 stops, which is workable with a tripod).

### Filter Usage Notes

- Infrared filters appear nearly **completely black** — you cannot compose through the viewfinder. The correct approach is to compose and focus without the filter, then carefully attach it before shooting
- Avoid shaking the camera when mounting the filter, as this will shift focus
- Use a cable release or self-timer to trigger the shutter and minimize camera shake

## Shooting Techniques: Exposure and Focus

### Exposure Method

Exposure is one of the most challenging aspects of infrared photography. Here is the recommended workflow:

**Step 1: Determine baseline exposure**

Meter without the filter first. For example, using Ilford SFX 200, your normal meter reading gives f/8 at 1/125s.

**Step 2: Calculate filter compensation**

With an R72 filter attached, you need approximately 12 stops of compensation. Starting from 1/125s:

- 1/125 → 1/60 → 1/30 → 1/15 → 1/8 → 1/4 → 1/2 → 1s → 2s → 4s → 8s → 15s → 30s

This means at f/8, you need approximately 30 seconds of exposure time.

**Step 3: Bracket your exposures**

Infrared exposure is difficult to judge precisely — different scenes have vastly different infrared reflectance. **Bracketing is strongly recommended**, shooting 1-2 stops above and below your calculated value.

### The "Secret" of Focusing: Infrared Focus Shift

This is the most overlooked and most important technical issue in infrared photography.

**Infrared light focuses at a different point than visible light.** Because different wavelengths of light refract at different rates when passing through lens glass (dispersion), infrared light focuses slightly behind the visible light focal plane. This means that if you focus precisely under visible light and then shoot infrared, your images will likely be soft.

There are two solutions:

**Method 1: Use the infrared focus mark on your lens**

Many manual focus lenses (especially those from the 1970s-80s) have an additional focus mark on the focusing ring, usually a red dot or a red "R" letter. The procedure:

1. Compose and focus normally without the filter
2. Note the focus position
3. Rotate the focus ring to shift from the original position to the infrared mark
4. Attach the filter and shoot

The focus shift is usually small (about 1-3mm of focal displacement), but it will noticeably affect sharpness at wide apertures. Stopping down to f/8 or smaller increases depth of field and reduces dependence on precise focusing.

**Method 2: Use a small aperture**

Stop down to f/11 or f/16, using the greater depth of field to tolerate focus errors caused by the shift. This is the simpler and more reliable method, at the cost of longer exposure times.

### A Tripod Is Essential

Due to the long exposure times caused by infrared filters, handheld shooting is nearly impossible. **A sturdy tripod is essential equipment for infrared photography**. Also recommended:

- Cable release or remote trigger
- Mirror lock-up function (if available)
- Lens hood to prevent stray light from entering around the filter edge

## Composition and Creativity: The Aesthetics of the Infrared World

### Finding Infrared-Friendly Scenes

Not all scenes are equally suited to infrared photography. The following scene characteristics produce the strongest infrared effects:

- **Abundant green vegetation**: Leaves and grass turn bright white due to the Wood Effect, creating strong contrast against dark skies
- **Scenes with water**: Water absorbs infrared light, appearing dark and contrasting with bright foliage
- **Open scenes with sky**: Infrared light scatters less in the atmosphere, so skies appear deep and dark
- **Hazy or foggy conditions**: Infrared light penetrates haze better than visible light, so distant objects appear clearer than in normal photographs

### Classic Infrared Photography Subjects

- **Summer landscapes**: The season with the most abundant foliage produces the strongest infrared effects. Surreal scenes of white leaves against dark skies
- **Urban parks**: Green spaces within cities take on an entirely different appearance under infrared
- **Portraits**: Skin appears smooth and almost glowing under infrared, but eyes may turn dark (because the iris absorbs infrared light), requiring careful attention
- **Architecture combined with vegetation**: The contrast between man-made structures and natural elements is especially striking in infrared

### Black-and-White vs. Color Infrared

Black-and-white infrared films (such as Ilford SFX 200, Rollei IR 400) produce high-contrast monochrome images, with white foliage against dark skies as their signature look.

Color infrared (such as Kodak Aerochrome) produces false-color effects, creating dreamlike colors — this is not the result of post-processing, but rather the film's own chemical characteristics. Aerochrome's infrared-sensitive layer is recorded as red, the red-sensitive layer records blue, and the overall effect resembles an alien world.

## Processing and Scanning

### Processing Black-and-White Infrared Film

The good news is that most infrared black-and-white films can be processed using standard black-and-white chemistry:

- **Ilford SFX 200**: Use ID-11 or D-76 at normal development times. Some photographers recommend reducing development time by 10-15% to control contrast
- **Rollei Infrared 400**: Use standard black-and-white chemistry; pay attention to the PET base curling issue

### Processing Precautions

- **Protect from infrared light**: Film is very sensitive to infrared light before loading. Avoid loading near strong infrared sources (such as direct sunlight or infrared remote controls)
- **Base curling**: Rollei IR 400's PET base is stiff and may curl after processing. Flatten it between sheet film holders while still damp, then air dry
- **Safe lights**: Infrared film has similar safe light requirements to regular black-and-white film, but due to its additional infrared sensitivity, it is recommended to work in complete darkness

### Scanning

Once processed, scanning is no different from regular film. It is recommended to scan in 48-bit color (even for black-and-white infrared film) to retain maximum tonal information. After scanning, you can further adjust contrast and tone in Photoshop or Lightroom.

## Digital Alternatives and the Case for Film Infrared

Digital infrared conversion (removing the camera sensor's IR-cut filter) is well established and can achieve effects similar to infrared film. But film infrared photography retains irreplaceable advantages:

- **Unique tonal response**: The emulsion's response curve to infrared light differs from digital sensors, producing more natural tonal transitions
- **Unpredictability**: Each roll of infrared film yields subtly different results, and this unpredictability is itself part of the creative process
- **Complete chemical process**: From exposure to development, the infrared image is produced entirely by photochemical reactions, with no digital processing involved
- **Aerochrome's false color**: To this day, no digital solution has perfectly replicated Aerochrome's false-color effect

## Advice for Beginners

If you have never tried infrared photography, here is a progressive path to get started:

1. **Start with Ilford SFX 200 + a deep red filter**: You don't need an expensive infrared filter — a standard deep red filter (such as Heliopan 715nm) will let you experience infrared effects
2. **Shoot landscapes first**: Green plants are the easiest subjects to demonstrate infrared effects
3. **Use a tripod**: Even if exposure times are not extremely long, a stable platform ensures sharpness
4. **Record your settings for every shot**: The trial-and-error cost of infrared photography is high; detailed notes help you learn from experience
5. **Try bracketing**: Shoot one frame above and one below your calculated exposure to find the optimal result
6. **Start with small apertures**: f/11 or f/16 will tolerate focus errors and reduce technical difficulty
7. **Shoot in summer**: The season with the most abundant foliage and strongest sunlight produces the best infrared effects

## Conclusion

Infrared film photography is a unique and fascinating branch of the film world. It lets you see a world invisible to the naked eye — white leaves, deep black skies, glowing skin — these surreal images are not the product of post-processing, but the direct result of light and chemistry.

In an age where everything can be digitally simulated, infrared film photography reminds us that the essence of photography is **writing with light**. And the range of light is far broader than what our eyes can see.

Pick up your camera, load a roll of infrared film, and look at the world through an R72 filter — you will discover that everything familiar becomes strangely unfamiliar, and within that strangeness lies a beauty that is utterly captivating.
