## An Emulsion Born for a Specific Series

On April 10, 2026, Kodak officially launched KODAK VERITA 200D Color Negative Film. This was not a routine product line update—it is the first entirely new motion picture color negative emulsion in nearly a decade, and from the very beginning of the project it had a clear creative objective: to provide a film stock that had never existed before for the 65mm cinematography of HBO's Euphoria Season 3.

Cinematographer Marcell Rév and director Sam Levinson, while preparing Season 3, wanted a daytime image quality distinctly different from the existing VISION3 series. Rév described the look he wanted as "less sensitive, looks a little more like an old school, golden age film print stock"—lower sensitivity, more reminiscent of a golden-age release print. This request directly catalyzed a targeted development effort by Kodak's emulsion engineering team.

## Technical Specifications and Emulsion Design Logic

VERITA 200D is a 200ASA daylight-balanced color negative film available in 65mm, 35mm, and 16mm formats, processed using standard ECN-2 chemistry. In terms of specifications, 200ASA sits in the mid-to-low sensitivity range for motion picture film—lower than VISION3 250D (5207) and significantly lower than VISION3 500T (5219). But it is precisely this "not sensitive enough" characteristic that constitutes its core aesthetic value.

**Technical Point 1: The Relationship Between Low Sensitivity and Grain Structure.** In silver halide emulsion systems, sensitivity is positively correlated with crystal size—higher ISO means larger silver halide grains to capture more photons. By pushing sensitivity down to 200ASA, VERITA 200D achieves smaller, more densely packed silver halide crystals in its emulsion layers, resulting in finer grain structure and smoother tonal transitions. This aligns closely with the "golden-age release print" quality Rév pursued—films of that era were indeed generally lower in sensitivity with tighter grain.

**Technical Point 2: The Synergy Between 65mm Format and Emulsion.** The image area of 65mm film is approximately 2.5 times that of standard 35mm (5-perf 65mm effective frame is approximately 48.5×22mm versus 35mm's approximately 24×18mm). A larger negative area means lower final magnification, further reducing the visibility of grain on screen. VERITA 200D's fine-grain emulsion combined with the large-format advantage of 65mm maintains extremely high resolution and minimal visible grain even under 4K or 8K scanning—precisely the demanding image quality requirements of HDR large-screen viewing in the streaming era.

**Technical Point 3: The Warm-Cool Dual-Stock Workflow.** Rév established a clear dual-film system for Euphoria Season 3: daytime scenes shot on VERITA 200D (daylight-balanced, 5500K), nighttime scenes on VISION3 500T (tungsten-balanced, 3200K). This is not merely an exposure strategy choice but a color narrative tool—VERITA 200D renders warm, saturated, slightly nostalgic tones under daylight, while 500T retains cool cyan-blue tendencies under tungsten and neon lighting. The color science differences between the two emulsions are consciously woven into the series' visual narrative, creating emotional contrast between day and night.

## From Kodachrome to VERITA: The Kodak Emulsion Evolution

The birth of VERITA 200D did not come from nowhere—it sits on the extension line of Kodak's 137 years of emulsion engineering. In 1889, Kodak introduced the first commercial transparent roll film, establishing the foundation of flexible film base; in 1935, Kodachrome debuted as the first commercially successful color reversal film, its complex K-14 processing and exceptional archival stability remaining the benchmark of film color science to this day; in 1948, safety base (cellulose acetate and later polyester) completely replaced the highly flammable nitrocellulose base, eliminating the greatest physical threat to film archives; in the 2000s, the VISION3 series pushed motion picture color negative latitude beyond 14 stops with advanced T-Grain (tabular grain) technology and multi-layer emulsion structures.

VERITA 200D's unique position in this lineage lies in the fact that it was not designed to pursue higher sensitivity, wider latitude, or finer grain—these are the classic directions of traditional emulsion iteration. Its design objective is a specific "visual personality," a contemporary engineering recreation of golden-age image quality. This marks a shift in motion picture film development logic from "performance metrics-driven" to "creative intent-driven."

## Manufacturer-Creator Co-Development: A New Paradigm for the Film Industry?

Kodak positions VERITA 200D as an "innovative pilot stock"—a phrase that itself implies a new product logic: rather than releasing universal products for the entire industry, designing emulsions tailored to specific projects by specific creators.

This model is not entirely without precedent in the film industry—Kodak has long provided custom-batch films for major studios, but historically these customizations were typically fine-tuning of existing emulsions (adjusting sensitivity, shifting color balance) rather than designing entirely new emulsion structures from scratch. What makes VERITA 200D different is that it is a completely new emulsion with its own product number and a public launch, its very creation becoming part of the marketing narrative.

For Kodak, this strategy carries multiple values: it elevates film from "consumable" to "creative tool," strengthens bonds with top-tier creators, and through culturally significant projects like Euphoria, gives film sustained public visibility. At the 2025 Cannes Film Festival, 24 selected productions were shot on Kodak film, including works by Wes Anderson, Spike Lee, and Kristen Stewart—film's presence in high-end production has not faded.

## Elite Craft or Mass Medium? A Cultural Question

However, as film emulsion development increasingly depends on the custom needs of a few top-tier film and television projects, an unavoidable question emerges: is film transitioning from a mass medium to an elite craft?

VERITA 200D targets the filmmaking market that uses 65mm cameras, has ample lighting budgets, and treats ECN-2 professional processing as standard. Ordinary film photography enthusiasts cannot directly use this stock—it has no 135 cassette format, no C-41 processing compatibility, and no exposure index calibration for reflected-light metering. Its existence essentially serves a very small community of creators.

But this does not mean it lacks significance for broader film culture. Just as Formula 1 technology eventually trickles down to production cars, the emulsion technology developed for Euphoria—finer grain control, more precise color balance—may influence consumer film products in some form in the future. More importantly, the very existence of VERITA 200D proves one thing: in 2026, dominated by streaming and digital photography, there are still those willing to invest R&D resources in a specific chemical image quality. Film has not been forgotten—it is simply needed in a different way.

From the first commercial roll film in 1889 to VERITA 200D in 2026, Kodak's emulsion engineering has traversed 137 years. Along this road there is the glory of Kodachrome, the danger of nitrate base, the precision of VISION3, and now a name born for a television series. The story of film is far from over—only its next chapter may no longer be written by the mass market, but co-defined by a few devoted creators and the engineers willing to formulate emulsions for them.
