## Introduction

That Nikon FM2, Canon AE-1, or Pentax K1000 in your hands has probably seen you through countless shutter clicks. Its mechanical innards are precise and elegant — every press of the shutter release is a symphony of gears, springs, and aperture blades. But have you ever wondered: when these classic machines break down, who is left to fix them?

This is not a hypothetical question. In late 2025, an ABC News report sparked widespread discussion across the film photography community — veteran film camera repair technicians with decades of experience are retiring en masse, and the repair knowledge and craftsmanship for many classic models faces the risk of being lost forever. At the same time, the film photography revival is bringing ever more people back to analog cameras. Supply is shrinking while demand is growing, and this tension is shaping the future of the film camera repair industry.

This article covers daily maintenance, diagnosing common problems, understanding CLA service, knowing the boundaries of DIY repair, and finding a professional technician — a practical guide to keeping your film camera running.

## Daily Maintenance: Prevention Always Beats Cure

### Storage Environment

What harms a film camera most is not use, but neglect. A camera that sits unused for long periods often develops more problems than one that is shot regularly.

**Humidity control** is the top priority for camera storage. Relative humidity between 40% and 50% is ideal. Too low and the leather shrinks and cracks while lubricants evaporate; too high and mold thrives, eating away at lens coatings and mechanical components. An electronic dry cabinet is the best solution. On a budget, a sealed container with silica gel desiccant works too — just remember to replace the desiccant regularly.

**Temperature** matters just as much. Never leave your camera in direct sunlight, inside a car, or next to a heater. Extreme heat accelerates the aging of rubber seals and can cause metering cells to fail.

**Batteries** are the most overlooked hazard. If your camera uses batteries (the Canon AE-1's metering system, for example, depends entirely on them), remove them when the camera will sit idle for an extended period. Alkaline battery leakage is devastating to circuit boards — once the corrosion seeps into the metering system or the shutter electromagnet, the repair cost can exceed the value of the camera itself.

### Usage Habits

Good habits go a long way toward extending a camera's life.

**Cycle the shutter and advance lever regularly**, even without film loaded. Shutters that haven't been fired for a long time tend to stick — especially leaf shutters, where the lubricant thickens and the blades close sluggishly or not at all. Fire the shutter a few times at 1/60s and 1/500s to keep the mechanism supple.

**Never force anything**. If the advance lever meets resistance, don't yank it — the film may have reached the end of the roll, or a gear may be damaged. Forcing the advance is the single most common cause of stripped advance gears.

**Keep it clean**. After each use, blow dust off the body and lens with a blower, and clean the glass with a lens pen or dedicated lens tissue. Sand grains lodged in the body seams can work their way inside with each advance stroke.

## Common Faults and How to Spot Them

Recognizing the symptoms of common problems helps you catch them before they get worse.

### Inaccurate Shutter Speeds

**Symptoms**: Consistently over- or underexposed frames, or high-speed shutters that audibly drag (a 1/1000s exposure that sounds like 1/30s). This is the most common issue in old cameras, usually caused by dried or degraded lubricant in the shutter curtains. When the cloth focal-plane shutter's oil dries out, the curtain travels unevenly or slowly.

### Metering Errors

**Symptoms**: The built-in meter's readings clearly disagree with a handheld meter or the Sunny 16 rule. Possible causes include incorrect battery voltage (many classic cameras were designed around now-discontinued mercury cells), aging metering cells, or poor circuit board contacts.

### Sticky Film Advance

**Symptoms**: The advance lever feels rough, makes unusual noises, or fails to wind the film to the next frame. This is usually caused by worn gears or degraded lubricant. If you see metal shavings, the gears are already significantly worn.

### Leaky Aperture Blades

**Symptoms**: An oily sheen on the aperture blades, or the blades closing sluggishly (or not at all) when stopping down. After years of use, lubricant migrates from the mechanical parts onto the blades. Oily blades not only affect exposure accuracy — they can also prevent the aperture from closing to the set value during exposure, resulting in a wider-than-intended aperture.

### Foggy Viewfinder

**Symptoms**: The viewfinder image is always blurry, even with the focus ring set to infinity. Usually caused by a deteriorating focusing screen or dust inside the viewfinder. This doesn't affect the actual image on film, but it makes precise focusing difficult.

## CLA: The Full Service Your Camera Deserves

### What Is CLA

CLA stands for **Clean, Lubricate, Adjust** — the three pillars of professional film camera maintenance. A complete CLA typically includes:

- **Clean**: Disassemble the camera to remove internal dust, old lubricant, and debris. The optical surfaces of the focusing screen, mirror, and viewfinder prism are carefully cleaned.
- **Lubricate**: Reapply specialist lubricants to critical moving parts — the shutter mechanism, advance gears, mirror assembly, and more. Different components require lubricants of different viscosities and formulations, which is one of the most technically demanding aspects of a CLA.
- **Adjust**: Calibrate shutter speeds, focus accuracy, metering system, and film transport to bring the camera back to factory specifications.

### How Often Should You Get a CLA

It depends on how often you shoot and how the camera is stored. As a general guideline:

- **Regularly used cameras**: Every 3–5 years
- **Occasionally used cameras**: Every 5–8 years
- **Newly acquired second-hand cameras**: Get a CLA as soon as possible — you have no way of knowing how the previous owner maintained it

Not every camera needs a full CLA. If your shutter speeds are accurate, the advance is smooth, and the meter reads correctly, a basic inspection and cleaning may be all that's needed.

## DIY Repair: What You Can (and Can't) Do

### Safe to Do Yourself

**Clean the viewfinder and focusing screen**. On most SLRs, you can blow dust off the focusing screen through the lens mount. Never use liquids or cloth on the focusing screen — its surface is extremely delicate, and even a light scratch will be permanently visible.

**Battery substitutes**. Many classic cameras (the Leica M3's meter, the Nikon F2's metering prism) were designed for 1.35V mercury cells (PX625, PX13, etc.), which are no longer produced for environmental reasons. Alternatives include: 1.55V silver oxide cells with a voltage adapter (such as a WeinCell), zinc-air batteries (voltage is stable but lifespan is shorter), or simply using a handheld meter or a phone metering app.

**Replace the light seals**. A universal ailment of old cameras — the foam light seals around the film door and back cover degrade into a sticky mess over time, causing light leaks. Replacing them is a relatively straightforward DIY job. Seal kits are available online for most popular models. The key is using foam of the correct thickness and ensuring a tight fit at the joints.

### Better Left to a Professional

**Shutter speed calibration**. This requires specialized equipment and years of experience. Attempting it yourself is a fast way to make the problem worse.

**Lens disassembly**. The optical elements in a lens are precision-machined, and reassembly accuracy is very hard to guarantee. In multi-group, multi-element lenses, even a tiny misalignment noticeably degrades image quality.

**Electronic component repair**. Circuit boards in old cameras rarely have off-the-shelf replacement parts. Proper repair demands electronics expertise and soldering skills.

**Mirror and prism work**. The coatings on these optical elements are extremely fragile — improper cleaning can cause irreversible damage.

## Twilight of the Repair Technicians

This is the heaviest, but also the most important, section of this article.

According to the ABC News report in late 2025, film camera repair technicians with decades of experience are retiring at an accelerating pace worldwide. Most of these technicians entered the trade in the 1970s and 1980s, worked in manufacturer-authorized service centers, and know the mechanical intricacies of specific brands inside out. Their retirement means not only the loss of skilled hands but also a vast body of tacit knowledge — diagnosing shutter tension by the sound of its release, sensing gear wear by the feel of the advance lever.

Organizations like Camera Rescue have pointed out that this "knowledge gap" poses a real threat to the entire film photography ecosystem. A Hasselblad 500C that no one can repair is, in essence, an expensive paperweight.

But the outlook is not entirely bleak. In Reddit's Analog Community and various forums, a growing number of younger technicians are sharing repair experiences and tutorials. Organizations like Camera Rescue are actively pushing technician training programs, striving to pass on knowledge before the older generation fully retires. YouTube and other platforms are filled with detailed camera teardown and repair videos, lowering the barrier to self-learning.

## How to Find a Reliable Repair Technician

When you've confirmed that your camera needs professional attention, here are some tips:

**Prefer specialists**. Film camera mechanics vary widely by brand — a technician who excels at Nikon may not be the best fit for a Leica or Pentax. Ask about their area of expertise before sending anything in.

**Ask what the CLA covers and what warranty is offered**. A reputable CLA service should clearly list what's included and provide a warranty period (typically 3–6 months).

**Lean on community reputation**. Reddit's r/AnalogCommunity, film photography forums, and online communities are full of user-shared repair experiences and technician recommendations. Word of mouth is the best gauge of reliability.

**Expect a wait**. Good technicians are usually booked months in advance. Don't let impatience push you toward a faster but less reputable option.

## Give Your Camera a Future

Every film camera is a microcosm of engineering history. They were built in an era before planned obsolescence — with proper care, a 1960s Nikon F can keep working indefinitely.

As users, the three most basic things we can do are: store it properly, keep it active, and maintain it on schedule. When a problem is beyond our own skill, find a trustworthy technician and hand the camera over. You're not just maintaining a tool — you're supporting a community of craftspeople racing against time.

Next time you press the shutter, listen to that sound. It's the meshing of gears, the curtain sweeping across the focal plane, the mirror flipping up and falling back. That sound deserves to keep being heard.
