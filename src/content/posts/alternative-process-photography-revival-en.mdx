## Introduction

At a time when AI can generate a "perfect" photograph in seconds, a group of photographers has chosen a radically different path — they coat cotton paper with iron salt solutions, dip glass plates in collodion, crush flower petals onto canvas, and wait for sunlight to slowly write the image.

This is neither nostalgic performance nor behavioral art. This is the Alternative Photographic Processes revival movement happening worldwide in 2026. From workshops in New York to independent galleries in Tokyo, from the #cyanotype hashtag on Instagram to hand-darkroom courses across Europe, an increasing number of photographers are using 19th-century techniques to create 21st-century images.

This article will guide you through six of the most active historical photographic processes, exploring their history, principles, and contemporary practice.

## Cyanotype: Images Written by Sunlight in Prussian Blue

### Two Geniuses and a Blue Miracle

The cyanotype process was invented in 1842 by Sir John Herschel, a British astronomer and chemist. The principle is straightforward: mix ferric ammonium citrate with potassium ferricyanide, coat the solution onto paper or fabric, and once dry, the surface becomes light-sensitive. When ultraviolet light (sunlight) hits the treated surface, the iron salts undergo a photochemical reaction, producing insoluble Prussian blue precipitate.

Areas blocked by objects retain their original color, and after washing with water, the result reveals the iconic blue-and-white image.

### The First Female Photographer

Herschel's friend, the botanist Anna Atkins, quickly recognized the potential of cyanotype. In 1843, she used this process to create *Photographs of British Algae: Cyanotype Impressions*, placing seaweed specimens directly onto sensitized paper for exposure, producing the first photographic book in human history.

Atkins' work demonstrated an important truth: photography was never just about "taking pictures with a camera." It is a way of **using light to record the world**, and the camera is merely one of many tools.

### Why Cyanotype Is Thriving Today

Cyanotype has become the most popular entry point in the alternative process revival for several reasons:

- **Accessible materials**: Both primary chemicals are available at art supply stores or online, and they are non-toxic
- **No darkroom needed**: Sunlight serves as the exposure source — you don't even need a camera
- **High tolerance for error**: Exposure times range from minutes to hours, making success easy for beginners
- **Unique results**: Every cyanotype has its own shade of blue, texture, and character that cannot be perfectly replicated

In recent years, many artists have combined cyanotype with contemporary themes — some use it to document traces of climate change, others create personal archive projects, and fashion designers incorporate cyanotype fabrics into their collections. In 2025, the "Unique: Alternative Processes" international photography competition received a record number of entries, with cyanotype works making up the largest share.

## Wet Plate Collodion & Tintype

### The Victorian Era's "Snapshot" Technology

In 1851, British sculptor Frederick Scott Archer published the wet plate collodion process. This technique dissolved nitrocellulose (collodion) in ether and alcohol, added potassium iodide, coated it onto a glass plate, dipped it while still wet into silver nitrate solution to make it light-sensitive, then immediately loaded it into the camera for exposure and development.

The entire process had to be completed before the collodion dried — typically a working window of only 10 to 15 minutes. This meant photographers needed to carry a complete mobile darkroom with them.

### From Ambrotype to Tintype

The wet plate process had two main presentation formats:

- **Ambrotype**: The image appears on a glass plate, viewed as a positive through a black backing
- **Tintype (Ferrotype)**: The image appears directly on a blackened iron plate, more durable and less expensive

The tintype method reached its peak in America during the 1860s. During the American Civil War, traveling photographers used tintypes to create soldier portraits — these small metal photographs became the final memento for many families.

### Contemporary Wet Plate Practitioners

Today, wet plate photography has not only survived but formed a vibrant international community. John Coffer in the United States is an iconic figure in this field, having practiced wet plate since the 1970s and teaching workshops to pass on the craft. The website Modern Tintype documents how a new generation of photographers brings this ancient technique into contemporary contexts.

The appeal of wet plate photography lies in its **materiality** — each tintype is a unique physical object, not a file that can be infinitely reproduced. The image is embedded in the metal surface, possessing a depth and texture that digital prints simply cannot achieve.

## Gum Bichromate: The Boundary Between Photography and Painting

### Photographs You Can "Paint"

The gum bichromate process was born in the mid-19th century, but its golden age was the Pictorialism movement from the 1890s to the 1910s. The core philosophy of this process is: **photographs don't have to look like photographs**.

The process works as follows: mix gum arabic with potassium dichromate, add watercolor pigment, and coat onto paper. Once dry, place a negative on top for contact printing. The exposed gum hardens, while unexposed areas can be washed away with water.

The key is that during the washing process, the photographer can **manually control** the degree of pigment removal with a brush, creating effects similar to watercolor or pastel paintings. The same negative can produce completely different prints — each one unique.

### The Contemporary Revival of Gum Printing

Gum printing was nearly forgotten by the mid-20th century, but over the past decade it has regained photographers' interest. Its appeal lies in breaking the prejudice that "photography is mechanical reproduction" — every gum print bears the maker's hand, existing somewhere between photography and painting.

## Anthotype: Photographing with Flowers

### The Oldest "Green" Photographic Process

Anthotype may be the most environmentally friendly of all photographic processes. The principle is simple: crush colored flower petals, leaves, or berries to extract their juice, coat it onto paper. Natural pigments like anthocyanins are photosensitive and gradually fade under sunlight. Covering parts of the surface with opaque objects or negatives creates color contrast after exposure.

This process was first documented by British scientist John Herschel in the 1830s, but because plant juices have extremely low sensitivity (exposure may take days or even weeks), it never became a mainstream photographic method.

### Anthotype in Contemporary Art

Yet it is precisely this "slowness" and "uncontrollability" that has found new ground for anthotype in contemporary art. Many eco-artists use it to explore the relationship between humans and nature — using the plant's own pigments to record the plant's image is itself a poetic cycle.

In 2026, multiple photography workshops feature anthotype as a core course, where participants can collect flowers outdoors, make sensitized paper on the spot, and complete exposure in sunlight. The entire process requires no chemicals and no electricity.

## Albumen Print: The Dominant Image of the 19th Century

### The Image Revolution in Egg Whites

Albumen printing was the world's dominant photographic printing method from the 1850s to the 1890s. The principle uses egg white (albumen) as a carrier for light-sensitive chemicals — mix albumen with sodium chloride, coat onto paper, dry, then dip in silver nitrate solution to sensitize, and finally contact print with a negative.

Albumen prints produce warm tones and rich detail, ranging from deep chocolate brown to soft rose. The vast majority of 19th-century original photographs we see today — including Mathew Brady's American Civil War photographs and Julia Margaret Cameron's portraits — are albumen prints.

### The Return of Contemporary Albumen Printing

Although albumen printing is far more complex than cyanotype, a group of photographers have recently re-mastered this technique. What they pursue is the unique tone and texture of albumen prints — that warm brownish-red hue that silver gelatin papers and digital prints simply cannot replicate.

## Why We Still Need Hand Printing in 2026

### Slow Practice Against "Immediacy"

We live in an age of image excess. Billions of digital photos are produced globally every day, and AI-generated images have pushed image production speed to unprecedented levels. Against this backdrop, the significance of hand printing lies not in "better images" but in a **different experience of image-making**.

When you spend two hours mixing chemicals, coating paper, waiting for sunlight exposure, and watching an image slowly emerge in a tray of water, your understanding of "taking a photograph" fundamentally changes. Photography ceases to be a split-second action and becomes an extended duration.

### The Return of Materiality

Digital photos have no weight, no smell, no texture. A cyanotype has the paper's grain, the scent of chemicals, the trace of sunlight. A tintype is cold and heavy in your hand. These material properties transform images from pixels on a screen into tangible objects.

### Community and Transmission

The revival of alternative photographic processes is not merely a technical regression — it is a social phenomenon. Workshops, online forums (such as AlternativePhotography.com), and annual exhibitions (like the Unique: Alternative Processes competition) worldwide provide spaces for learning and exchange. In these communities, knowledge is shared face-to-face, hand-by-hand — itself a gentle rebellion against the "remote collaboration" of the digital age.

## How to Start Your First Hand Print

If historical photographic processes have sparked your interest, here is the gentlest path to begin:

### Start with Cyanotype

1. **Purchase materials**: Buy a cyanotype kit online (typically includes Solution A and Solution B), usually priced between $15-30
2. **Choose your substrate**: Watercolor paper is the most common choice, but cotton fabric, wood panels, or even leaves work
3. **Coat the solution**: Use a sponge or brush to apply equal parts of Solution A and B evenly onto paper, dry in darkness
4. **Prepare materials**: Collect botanical specimens, paper cutouts, negatives, or other opaque objects
5. **Expose**: Place your materials on the coated paper, press under a sheet of glass, expose to sunlight for 5-20 minutes
6. **Wash**: Rinse with water for 5 minutes — the image will shift from yellow-green to Prussian blue
7. **Dry**: Air dry, then iron at low temperature to flatten

The entire process requires no darkroom, no camera, and no professional equipment. All you need is sunlight, water, and a little patience.

## Conclusion

The revival of historical photographic processes is not a rejection of digital technology, but an expansion of what images can be. When the Prussian blue of cyanotype slowly appears under sunlight, when a tintype image emerges from the collodion, when flower juice slowly fades over days of exposure — what we see is not just the image itself, but the long and subtle conversation between time, light, and matter.

In an age that pursues "faster, clearer, more perfect" above all else, these ancient processes remind us: the value of an image lies not only in how it looks, but in how it was made.
