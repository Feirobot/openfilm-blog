## Introduction

In an age where AI can generate a flawless image in seconds, a growing number of young people have chosen the most "inefficient" way to take photographs — shooting on film.

This is no simple wave of nostalgia. In 2026, the global film photography industry is valued at $613 million and projected to reach $724 million by 2035. Between 2020 and 2026, bulk purchasing volumes climbed 127%. Over 25% of film buyers are under 35, and among shooters under 25, 67% say they choose film because it "forces you to slow down and shoot with intention."

These figures tell more than a market story. They point to a deeper cultural shift.

## The Anti-AI Authenticity Wave

One of the most fascinating drivers of the film revival is an instinctive rebellion against AI-generated imagery.

When AI can produce infinitely smooth, pixel-perfect images, the grain, unpredictable color shifts, and occasional light leaks of film have become synonymous with "real." Grain is the physical product of silver halide crystals — not a software simulation. Every frame is a genuine interaction between light and chemistry at a specific moment in time, impossible to replicate or mass-generate.

As one photographer put it: in a world where AI is everywhere, every imperfection on a roll of film is evidence of human presence.

This pursuit of authenticity is reshaping the standards by which we judge photography. On social media, more and more creators are sharing unretouched film scans — not because they are "perfect," but precisely because they are not. That imperfection has become a manifesto against algorithmic aesthetics.

## From "Special Occasions" to Everyday Documentation

Film used to be reserved for special moments — weddings, travel, artistic projects. But one of the defining trends of 2026 is film's return to daily life.

More and more photographers are using film to document ordinary days: the light on their commute, the lunch table, the weather outside the window. The logic behind this shift is simple — when you have only 36 exposures, you observe the world more carefully instead of mindlessly pressing the shutter.

This experience of delayed gratification feels especially precious in the age of instant social media feedback. After shooting a roll, you must wait for it to be developed and scanned before seeing the results. That wait is not a flaw — it is part of the experience itself, turning photography back into a process that demands patience and anticipation.

This has also driven demand for affordable film stocks. Sales of budget-friendly options like Kodak Gold 200 and Fujifilm C200 continue to climb, and Lucky Film's C200 emulsion sold out its initial run of 10,000 rolls almost immediately.

## A Full Supply Chain Recovery

Rising demand is fueling expansion across the entire supply chain.

**Manufacturing**: Eastman Kodak has invested $60 million to expand coating capacity at its Rochester plant, boosting output by 18%. Harman, Ilford's parent company, is also investing heavily with a goal of doubling annual production, and has launched experimental color films like Harman Red, Phoenix I and II, alongside Kentmere Pan 200 black-and-white film. Lomography continues to release new chemical formulations and reusable point-and-shoot cameras.

**Processing and scanning**: In 2025, 312 new processing and scanning labs opened worldwide — one every 28 hours on average. Most of these new labs offer hybrid scan packages (negatives + digital files), and geographic coverage has expanded into South Korea, Brazil, Indonesia, and Southeast Asia. For most photographers, dropping off a roll of film for processing has never been easier.

**Equipment**: The Pentax 17 half-frame camera sold out immediately upon release, and Canon showcased a film-style prototype at CP+ 2026. On the secondhand market, classic models like the Canon AE-1 have stabilized in the $100–$150 range — trading volumes are still climbing, but the price bubble is deflating.

## Popular Film Sales Trends (2020–2023)

A few data points illustrate the market's momentum:

- **Kodak Portra 400**: Sales up 156%, the go-to choice for professional portraiture
- **Kodak Gold 200**: Up 118%, the high-value pick for everyday shooting
- **Ilford HP5 Plus**: Consistently the top-selling black-and-white film, renowned for its latitude — shoot at box speed for classic tonal range, or push-process for more grain and contrast
- **Global distribution**: Surpassed 20 million rolls in 2023, a 15% year-over-year increase

## Advice for Aspiring Film Photographers

If you are thinking about getting into film photography, here is some practical advice:

**On cameras**: Start with a simple manual camera. The Pentax K1000 ($75–$125), Nikon FM ($100–$150), and Canon F-1 ($125–$200) are all classics. Prioritize models that run on common batteries like 1.5V button cells — avoid cameras that depend on discontinued specialty batteries, which can mean calibration headaches.

**On lenses**: A 50mm f/1.8 or f/2 standard lens is all you need to start. There is no need to chase expensive glass — what matters is learning to think in light.

**On film**: Begin with an affordable color negative like Kodak Gold 200 or Fujifilm C200. Get comfortable with the basics before moving on to premium stocks like Portra 400. For black and white, Ilford HP5 Plus is the best entry-level choice for home development.

**On mindset**: Do not fall for the idea that you need an expensive camera to take good photos. The heart of film photography is not the price tag on your gear — it is the way you see the world. Learn the Sunny 16 rule, and you will be fine even when your light meter fails.

## Closing Thoughts

The film revival of 2026 is no passing fad. As one industry observer noted: "This is not a hipster trend or a six-month TikTok moment."

When AI pushes the cost of image generation toward zero, the value of film is rising — not because it is more "convenient" or "efficient," but because it reminds us that the essence of photography is not producing images, but seeing the world. Every roll of film is an exercise: in patience, in focus, in truly noticing the light in front of you before you press the shutter.

That may be the most important thing about film in 2026.
