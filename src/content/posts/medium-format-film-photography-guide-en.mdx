## Introduction

If you've shot a few rolls of 135 film and started feeling that the 24×36mm frame is limiting your expression — perhaps grain appears too quickly when enlarging, the creamy bokeh you crave never quite materializes, or you simply long for a slower, more deliberate shooting rhythm — then medium format might be your next step.

Medium format film cameras use 120 film, with frame sizes ranging from 42×56mm (645 format) to 56×72mm (6×7 format). Even the smallest medium format frame is 2.7 times the area of 135 film, while the largest 6×7 frame reaches approximately 4.5 times. A larger negative means more detail, richer tonal gradation, shallower equivalent depth of field — and an entirely different shooting experience.

This article will build your complete understanding of medium format film photography across four dimensions: frame formats, camera types, classic models, and shooting techniques.

## Frame Formats: 645, 6×6, and 6×7 Each Have Their Strengths

### The 120 Film Format Family

120 film has a fixed width of 61mm, but different cameras utilize different imaging areas, creating three mainstream formats:

| Format | Actual Image Size | Frames per Roll | Frame Area (vs 135) | Representative Cameras |
|--------|------------------|-----------------|---------------------|----------------------|
| 645 | 42×56mm | 15 or 16 | ~2.7× | Pentax 67, Mamiya 645, Contax 645 |
| 6×6 | 56×56mm | 12 | ~3.7× | Hasselblad 500C/M, Rolleiflex 2.8F, Yashica Mat-124G |
| 6×7 | 56×72mm | 10 | ~4.5× | Mamiya RB67 / RZ67, Pentax 67, Fujifilm GW670 |

There are also less common formats like 6×9 (56×84mm, represented by the Fujifilm GW690) and 6×4.5 variants, but the three above are the absolute mainstream.

### How to Choose Your Format

**645 format** offers the closest experience to 135. With 15 frames per roll, you won't be changing film frequently, and camera size and weight remain manageable. The Pentax 67 and Mamiya 645 can both be hand-held, making them suitable for street and portrait photography. If you're transitioning from 135, 645 is the smoothest bridge.

**6×6 format** is unique for its square composition. The square format is somewhat counterintuitive — without landscape or portrait orientation, compositional weight falls entirely on geometric relationships and visual balance. The Hasselblad 500C/M and Rolleiflex TLR are the iconic 6×6 cameras. Square composition has distinctive expressive power in portraiture, still life, and architectural photography.

**6×7 format** is the choice for those pursuing ultimate image quality. The 56×72mm negative is 4.5 times that of 135, and scanning can yield astonishing detail and tonal range. But the trade-offs are significant: large camera bodies, heavy weight, only 10 frames per roll, and greater shutter vibration. A Mamiya RB67 with lens can easily exceed 3 kilograms, better suited to slow shooting on a tripod.

## Camera Types: The Operating Philosophies of TLR, SLR, and Rangefinder

### Twin Lens Reflex Cameras (TLR)

The TLR's structure is intuitive and elegant: the upper lens handles viewing, the lower lens handles exposure, and the two are linked. You look down into a waist-level finder to compose — a shooting posture that fundamentally changes your relationship with the subject. You're no longer "pointing a camera at someone" but rather "examining a small ground glass screen, head bowed."

**Representative Models:**

- **Rolleiflex 2.8F / 3.5F**: The pinnacle of TLR design. The Planar 80mm f/2.8 or Xenotar 75mm f/3.5 lenses deliver optical quality that remains impressive today. Build quality and reliability are exceptional, but so is the price — a well-preserved 2.8F can exceed ¥10,000.
- **Yashica Mat-124G**: The value choice. The Yashinon 80mm f/3.5 lens is excellent, and the built-in meter (though its accuracy should be taken with a grain of salt) is a convenience. Prices typically range from one-third to one-half that of a Rolleiflex.
- **Mamiya C330**: A TLR with interchangeable lenses, covering focal lengths from 55mm to 250mm. The most versatile medium format TLR available.

TLR limitations include parallax — the viewing and taking lenses have different optical axes, causing composition偏差 at close distances. High-end TLRs have parallax compensation mechanisms, but the most reliable solution is developing a habit of slightly adjusting composition upward when shooting at close range.

### Medium Format SLR Cameras

Medium format SLRs use a mirror and pentaprism (or waist-level finder) for "what you see is what you get" viewing, eliminating TLR parallax issues while supporting interchangeable lenses.

**Representative Models:**

- **Hasselblad 500C/M**: The totem of medium format photography. Modular design — body, lens, finder, and film back are all separate and freely combinable. The Planar 80mm f/2.8 is the standard "normal" lens, producing images that are solid and warm. Fully mechanical, it works without batteries. A complete 500C/M system (body + 80mm lens + waist-level finder + A12 back) typically ranges from ¥8,000 to ¥15,000 on the used market, depending on condition.
- **Mamiya RB67 / RZ67**: The workhorses of 6×7 format. The RB67 is fully mechanical; the RZ67 adds battery-powered electronic shutter control. Both support rotating backs for easy switching between horizontal and vertical compositions, with extensive lens lineups. Ideal for studio, landscape, and high-quality work.
- **Pentax 67**: Known as the "tank of medium format." Its外形 resembles an enlarged 135 SLR, with the most similar operating logic. The 105mm f/2.4 standard lens produces mesmerizing bokeh. The drawback is significant mirror vibration — below 1/60s, hand-held success rates drop considerably.
- **Contax 645**: The "modernist" of medium format. Autofocus, electronic shutter, Zeiss lenses — this is the closest medium format camera to a modern operating experience. The Carl Zeiss 80mm f/2 lens delivers outstanding sharpness and color rendition. However, electronic system reliability is a concern; once the mainboard fails, repair difficulty and cost are high.

### Medium Format Rangefinder and Fixed-Lens Cameras

If you value portability and quiet operation, medium format rangefinders or fixed-lens cameras deserve consideration.

- **Fujifilm GW670 / GW690 / GSW690**: Folding fixed-lens rangefinders with 90mm f/3.5 (GW67) or 105mm f/8 (GW690/GSW690) lenses. Compact when folded, they transform into proper 6×7 or 6×9 cameras when opened. Lens quality is excellent, operation straightforward.
- **Mamiya 7 / 7II**: 6×7 rangefinder cameras with extremely compact bodies. The 80mm f/4 standard lens is small but sharp. Ideal for travel and documentary photography. Used prices have risen steadily in recent years.
- **Hasselblad SWC**: A waist-level finder camera with a fixed wide-angle (38mm f/4.5 Biogon) lens, designed for architecture and landscape. No lens focusing (hyperfocal distance is fixed), operation is extremely simple.

## From 135 to Medium Format: What You Need to Adapt To

### Your Shooting Pace Will Slow Down

The manual operation流程 of medium format cameras is more involved than 135: advancing film requires a crank (not a quick lever), focusing travel is longer, and metering may require an external meter or phone app. A 12-frame roll of 6×6 film takes 20-30 minutes at a moderate pace.

This isn't a drawback — it's a feature. Medium format encourages you to **compose more carefully and treat each shutter press with greater intention**. When you have 12 frames instead of 36, you'll naturally tend to spend a few extra seconds thinking before each exposure.

### Focusing Precision Demands More

Medium format has shallower depth of field than 135 (at equivalent angle of view and aperture). This means less margin for focusing error — ground glass focusing must be precise. With a waist-level finder, ground glass brightness drops noticeably at smaller apertures, making focusing in low light challenging.

Solutions include: using a pop-up magnifier for focusing assistance, choosing brighter focusing screens (such as Hasselblad's Acute-Matte screen, though it's expensive), or shooting in well-lit conditions.

### Shutter Vibration Is a Real Physical Problem

Medium format camera mirrors and shutter mechanisms are larger and heavier, producing correspondingly greater vibration. The Pentax 67's mirror "slap" is loud enough to make subjects turn heads in quiet environments.

**Coping Strategies:**

- Minimum safe shutter speed should be at least the reciprocal of focal length. For an 80mm lens, no slower than 1/80s; for 105mm, no slower than 1/100s
- On a tripod, use mirror lock-up if your camera supports it
- When hand-holding, adopt a stable grip and press the shutter at the end of an exhale

### Cost per Frame Is Higher

120 film itself costs more than 135 (a roll of Kodak Portra 400 in 120 typically costs 30%-50% more than the 135 version), and with fewer frames per roll (12 for 6×6, 10 for 6×7), the per-frame cost rises significantly. Adding processing and scanning fees, medium format per-frame cost is roughly 2-3 times that of 135.

This doesn't mean medium format is "expensive" — rather, every frame deserves more serious attention. When cost becomes a constraint, it becomes a discipline.

## Classic Model Recommendations: Choices for Different Budgets

### Entry Level (Under ¥3,000)

- **Yashica Mat-124G**: The first-choice TLR for beginners. Good lens quality, simple operation, friendly price. Check for lens fungus and meter accuracy when buying used.
- **Mamiya C330 + 80mm f/2.8 Sekor**: Interchangeable-lens TLR, fully featured, solid build. Considerably larger and heavier than the Yashica.
- **Fujifilm GW670**: Folding rangefinder, good portability, sharp lens. Check the bellows for light leaks.

### Mid-Range (¥3,000-10,000)

- **Hasselblad 500C/M + 80mm f/2.8 Planar**: The classic medium format choice. The modular system can be expanded gradually. When purchasing, check shutter speed accuracy and mirror mechanism smoothness.
- **Mamiya RB67 Pro-S + Sekor 90mm f/3.8**: The value option for 6×7 format. Fully mechanical, highly reliable. The rotating back design is practical.
- **Pentax 67 + 105mm f/2.4**: The medium format SLR closest to 135 operation. Extensive lens lineup, high playability.

### High-End (Over ¥10,000)

- **Hasselblad 503CW + 80mm f/2.8 Planar**: The ultimate version of the 500 series, adding TTL metering and flash control.
- **Contax 645 + 80mm f/2**: Autofocus medium format with first-rate Zeiss optics.
- **Rolleiflex 2.8F**: The ceiling of TLR design. The Planar 80mm f/2.8's image quality is impeccable.

## Practical Medium Format Shooting Tips

### Film Selection

Medium format's large negative amplifies film characteristics — grain, color tendencies, and contrast traits are all more fully displayed.

- **Low-ISO films perform best**: Kodak Ektar 100 and Fujifilm Provia 400F are stunning on medium format. Ektar 100's ultra-fine grain and saturated colors reveal a level of detail on 6×6 or 6×7 negatives that 135 simply cannot match.
- **Portra series is the safe bet**: Portra 160/400's skin tone rendition and exposure latitude are especially impressive on medium format, ideal for portraits and everyday shooting.
- **Black and white film choices**: Ilford HP5 Plus (ISO 400) and Kodak Tri-X 400 show considerably finer grain on medium format than on 135. For ultimate fineness, Ilford Pan F (ISO 50) on 6×6 negative is simply stunning.

### Exposure Strategy

Medium format film (especially negative) has excellent latitude, but the principle of "**expose for the highlights, develop for the shadows**" is recommended. Incident metering (hand-held meter pointed toward the subject) is more reliable than reflective metering (camera built-in meter).

If using a waist-level finder without built-in metering, phone apps like LightMe or myLightMeter are useful, or carry a dedicated incident meter.

### Scanning and Post-Processing

Medium format negatives should be scanned with professional film scanners (such as Fuji Frontier, Noritsu, or Epson V850) rather than basic drugstore services. Medium format negatives contain far more information than 135, and high-quality scanning can fully leverage the large negative's resolution advantage.

For 6×7 and 6×9 negatives, the Epson V850's scanning resolution can capture incredible detail. After scanning, use VueScan or SilverFast software for fine control rather than relying on automatic modes.

## Conclusion

Medium format film photography isn't "better 135" — it's a different way of seeing. The larger negative brings more detail and shallower depth of field, but more importantly, it brings a change in rhythm: slower, more deliberate, more ritualistic.

When you look down into a Rolleiflex's waist-level finder, examining an inverted world on the ground glass, you'll find your attention to composition increases dramatically. When you have 12 frames instead of 36, each shutter press becomes "heavier." This weight is not a burden but a force that keeps you more present in the moment.

If you've found joy in 135 film, medium format won't disappoint — it simply amplifies that joy to another dimension.
