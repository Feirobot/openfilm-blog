Many film photographers, after mastering black-and-white darkroom enlargement, naturally ask: can I make color prints in the same darkroom? The answer is yes — the RA-4 process allows a home darkroom to produce archival-quality color prints, and the equipment requirements are lower than you might expect.

![RA-4 darkroom color printing workstation overview: enlarger, three-step chemical trays, thermometer, filter pack, and test strip](https://media.openfilm.cc/images/darkroom-color-printing-ra4/20260714-1.webp)

## From Black-and-White to Color: What Changes in the Darkroom

If you already have a darkroom running D-72 or a similar black-and-white developer, switching to RA-4 doesn't require rebuilding the entire space. There are three key changes:

**Total darkness instead of safelight working conditions.** Black-and-white enlargement can be done under a safelight (typically red or amber), but RA-4 color paper (such as Kodak Endura or Fujifilm Crystal Archive) is sensitive to virtually all visible light, including red. This means you must work in complete darkness during development — but the good news is that the stop, bleach-fix, and wash stages can all be performed under normal lighting, since the paper is no longer light-sensitive after development.

**Tighter temperature control.** Black-and-white enlargement tolerates developer temperature variations of about ±1°C, while RA-4 requires a development temperature of 35°C (95°F) with a tolerance of only ±0.3°C. A precise digital thermometer and a thermostatic water bath are necessary investments.

**Chemical lifespan management.** The capacity life of RA-4 chemistry (developer and bleach-fix) is more sensitive than black-and-white solutions. The developer oxidizes faster in air, and replenisher can extend its life, but for small-scale home use, following the manufacturer's rated capacity is the safest strategy.

## The RA-4 Chemical Process in Detail

RA-4 is a three-step process, each with a clear function:

### Step 1: Development (3 min 15 sec, 35°C)

The developer reduces exposed silver halide to metallic silver while allowing color couplers to react with the oxidized developer, generating cyan, magenta, and yellow dye images in three separate layers. The key to this stage is **precise timing** and **constant temperature**.

Every 1°C deviation in developer temperature causes a perceptible shift in color balance. In a home setup, the most practical solution is to place the developer container in a larger water bath, maintained by a thermostatic heater. Agitation follows a pattern of continuous stirring for the first 15 seconds, then flipping the tray every 30 seconds — unlike the continuous agitation used in black-and-white processing, as over-agitation increases grain.

### Step 2: Bleach-Fix (1 min 30 sec, 30–35°C)

The bleach-fix (blix) performs two tasks simultaneously: it bleaches away the metallic silver image and dissolves unexposed silver halide. This step is less sensitive to temperature and time, but the solution must be sufficiently fresh — exhausted blix leads to silver retention, causing fog or color casts in the print.

### Step 3: Wash and Dry (5–10 min, room temperature)

Washing removes residual chemicals. For archival preservation, use Kodak Hypo Clearing Agent or an equivalent washing aid, then wash for the manufacturer's recommended time. Avoid hot air drying — the resin coating (RC) on RA-4 paper deforms at excessive temperatures.

## Color Cast Control: The Core Challenge of Color Enlargement

Black-and-white enlargement only requires controlling contrast and density; color enlargement demands simultaneous balance across three color channels. A "correct" color print depends on three variables:

**Enlarger light source color temperature.** Cold light heads and condenser heads have different spectral distributions, directly affecting the paper's color response. Cold light heads tend toward blue, requiring more magenta filtration to compensate.

**Filter pack.** Color enlargers use yellow (Y), magenta (M), and cyan (C) filter packs to fine-tune the light color. Initial filter values typically start from the paper manufacturer's recommended starting point, then are adjusted based on test strip results. The rule of thumb is to adjust one color channel at a time, in steps of 5–10 units.

**Negative color balance.** Different color negative films have distinct orange mask characteristics. Kodak Portra 400 and Fujifilm Pro 400H have different mask tones, and switching films requires recalibrating the filter pack. This is why many darkroom photographers maintain a separate set of filter parameters for each film stock they commonly use.

A practical mnemonic for color cast correction is "add the complementary filter": if the test strip is too yellow, increase the yellow filter value (reducing the yellow light reaching the paper); if it's too magenta, increase the magenta filter value. This sounds counterintuitive, but filters work by subtracting light — increasing a filter value reduces that color of light, shifting the final image toward its complementary color.

## From Negative to Print: The Complete Workflow

A complete color enlargement workflow proceeds as follows:

1. **Select the negative.** Prioritize negatives with accurate exposure and sharp focus. The latitude of color negatives allows ±2 stops of exposure deviation, but extremely over- or underexposed negatives are harder to correct for color cast during enlargement.

2. **Make a contact sheet.** If you have multiple frames, use a contact sheet to select candidates. Color contact sheets also use the RA-4 process, but you can use cheaper paper or cut scraps.

3. **Set enlarger parameters.** Based on the film stock and enlarger light source type, start from the recommended filter values. When using a new combination for the first time, run a baseline test with a known well-exposed negative.

4. **Test strips.** Cut a strip from the paper edge and test 5–6 different exposure times. Evaluate under normal lighting — remember, RA-4 paper is light-sensitive before development, but developed test strips can be viewed under white light.

5. **Fine-tune filtration.** Adjust the filter pack based on test strip results. If overall exposure is correct but color is off, adjust only the filters without changing exposure time; if both need adjustment, change them in separate steps to avoid mixing variables.

6. **Final enlargement.** Once parameters are confirmed, expose the full sheet and process through the RA-4 workflow.

7. **Evaluate and archive.** Evaluate the finished print under standard illumination (D50 or D65). Record all enlargement parameters — filter values, exposure time, magnification, paper batch — for future reproduction.

## The Aesthetic Value of Color Enlargement

Digital printing can certainly substitute for darkroom color enlargement, but the RA-4 process has its own distinct aesthetic character. Endura paper's color gamut differs from inkjet prints — its midtone color transitions are smoother, and the highlights have a subtle "breathing" quality. This is not a nostalgic filter effect, but a physical property of the silver halide process.

More importantly, darkroom color enlargement is a "subtractive" process: you subtract specific wavelengths from white light using filters, producing a precise color balance across the paper's three emulsion layers. This physical-level color control is fundamentally different from the RGB value specification of digital printing. Understanding this distinction deepens your understanding of color itself — not as pixel values, but as light.
