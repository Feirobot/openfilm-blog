## Introduction

The film photography revival is no longer just a trend — it's becoming a lasting creative practice. In 2026, the global film market is valued at over $600 million, Kodak has invested $60 million to expand its Rochester coating capacity, Ilford's parent company Harman is aiming to double annual production, and a new film processing lab opens somewhere in the world every 28 hours.

But when you stand in front of the film shelf at your local camera shop or browse an online store, faced with names like Kodak Gold 200, Portra 400, Ilford HP5 Plus, and CineStill 800T, a practical question arises: **which roll should I pick?**

This guide will help you sort through the options across four dimensions: color characteristics, ISO speed, use cases, and budget.

## Color Negative Film: The Most Versatile Choice

Color negative film is by far the most mainstream and accessible type today. Processed with the C-41 method, virtually any film lab in the world can handle it.

### Entry Level: Kodak Gold 200 / ColorPlus 200

These two are Kodak's consumer-grade color negative offerings and the best starting point for film photography.

**Kodak Gold 200** is known for its warm tones, with skin tones leaning toward warm yellow — ideal for everyday shooting in daylight and portraits. Its grain is well-controlled at ISO 200, and it's one of the most affordable color negative films available. Sales grew 118% between 2020 and 2023, making it one of the most popular films of the revival era.

**Kodak ColorPlus 200** shares similar emulsion technology with Gold 200 but with slightly lower saturation and higher contrast. It's often described as a "more restrained" Gold 200 — if you prefer a subtler color palette, ColorPlus is an even more economical option.

### Step Up: Kodak UltraMax 400

When you need higher ISO for indoor or overcast conditions, **Kodak UltraMax 400** is the most practical upgrade. ISO 400 means you can shoot handheld in dimmer environments while maintaining relatively fine grain. Its color balance leans neutral-warm, with excellent exposure latitude — great for travel and street photography where you need to react quickly.

### Professional Grade: Kodak Portra Series

**Kodak Portra** is the benchmark choice for professional photographers, available in three speeds:

- **Portra 160**: The finest grain and most natural colors, ideal for studio work and refined portraiture
- **Portra 400**: The all-rounder that balances speed and quality — the go-to for portraits, weddings, and travel. Sales surged 156% between 2020 and 2023, making it the fastest-growing film stock
- **Portra 800**: Ultra-high speed designed for low-light environments. Grain is visible but colors remain accurate — perfect for night street photography and indoor events

The Portra family's shared strengths are exceptional skin tone reproduction and extremely wide exposure latitude — even one or two stops of over- or underexposure still yield usable images. The trade-off, of course, is the price: 2-3 times that of consumer-grade films.

### The Fujifilm Alternative: Fujifilm C200 / Fujicolor 200

**Fujifilm C200** is the main competitor to Kodak's consumer films. Where Kodak leans warm, Fujifilm's color signature tends cooler and greener — greens in foliage and blues in skies render with more vivid punch. If you prefer a "cleaner" and more transparent look, C200 is worth exploring.

## Cinema Film Adapted for Stills: CineStill

**CineStill** repackages Kodak Vision 3 motion picture film for C-41 processing, offering two variants:

- **CineStill 800T**: Tungsten-balanced (3200K) cinema film that renders a distinct blue cast in daylight but produces accurate colors under tungsten lighting. Its most iconic feature is the halation effect — a red glow around highlights caused by removing the remjet anti-halation layer, creating a look unique to this film
- **CineStill 50D**: Daylight-balanced (5500K) cinema film with extremely fine grain and precise color reproduction, suited for photographers chasing that "cinematic" aesthetic

CineStill commands a premium (typically 3-4 times the price of standard color negative), but the images it produces are genuinely unlike anything else.

## Black and White Film: Back to Basics

Black and white film is more than a technical choice — it's a shift in how you see. When color is stripped away, light, tone, composition, and texture become the entire picture.

### The All-Rounder: Ilford HP5 Plus

**Ilford HP5 Plus** (ISO 400) is the world's best-selling black and white film and the recommended starting point for B&W photography. Its core strength is extraordinary exposure latitude — you can shoot it at ISO 400 for rich tonal range, or push it to ISO 1600 or even 3200 for increased grain and contrast. This flexibility makes it suitable for almost any scenario: street, portrait, landscape, documentary.

HP5 Plus's classic rival is **Kodak Tri-X 400**. The two perform similarly but with different character: Tri-X has coarser grain, higher contrast, and a more "traditional B&W" look; HP5 Plus is finer-grained with richer tonal gradation.

### Fine-Grain Choice: Ilford FP4 Plus / Kodak T-Max 100

When you need finer detail, **Ilford FP4 Plus** (ISO 125) and **Kodak T-Max 100** are the way to go. They excel in well-lit environments for portraiture, still life, and architectural photography — grain is nearly invisible and tonal transitions are exceptionally smooth.

### Low-Light Weapon: Ilford Delta 3200

**Ilford Delta 3200** is designed for extreme low-light situations. Although rated at ISO 3200, many photographers prefer to shoot it at ISO 1600 or 2500 for better tonal range. The grain is pronounced — and that's not a flaw, it's a feature. In dim bars, nighttime streets, or indoor concerts, Delta 3200 lets you capture images without flash, with the grain itself becoming part of the atmosphere.

## Budget Reference

In 2026, approximate price ranges for 35mm film (36 exposures):

- **Budget** ($6-9): Kodak Gold 200, ColorPlus 200, Fujifilm C200
- **Mid-range** ($10-15): Kodak UltraMax 400, Ilford HP5 Plus, Ilford FP4 Plus
- **Professional** ($15-22): Kodak Portra 160/400, Ilford Delta 3200
- **Specialty** ($20-28): Kodak Portra 800, CineStill 800T/50D

Keep in mind that film prices have risen steadily in recent years. Buying in bulk (10 rolls or more) typically yields better per-roll pricing. Also factor in processing and scanning costs when budgeting — C-41 color processing is usually slightly more expensive than black and white, and CineStill may incur additional charges at some labs due to the special remjet removal step.

## Advice for Beginners

If you're just starting out in film photography, here are a few practical tips:

**Start with one film**. Don't buy ten different rolls right away. Pick one (Kodak Gold 200 or Ilford HP5 Plus are both great choices), shoot 5-10 rolls, and learn its character thoroughly — how it responds to different light, its color tendencies, its latitude limits.

**Look for expired film**. Expired film often costs a third to half the price of fresh stock. Colors may shift and speed may drop, but these "flaws" are part of film aesthetics. Many iconic film photographs were made on expired stock.

**Log your shooting settings**. Note the lighting conditions, meter readings, and film type for every roll. Compare your notes with the developed results — this is the fastest way to learn how a particular film behaves.

## Conclusion

There is no "best film" — only the film that best fits your current scenario and creative intent. Kodak Gold 200's warmth and affordability, Portra 400's professionalism and reliability, HP5 Plus's versatility and forgiveness, CineStill 800T's unique cinematic feel — each film has its own personality, waiting for you to discover it through shooting.

2026 is a great time to start film photography: more choices than ever, a more mature supply chain, and lessons about light, patience, and observation that no digital post-processing can replicate.

Load a roll, put it in your camera, and go shoot.
